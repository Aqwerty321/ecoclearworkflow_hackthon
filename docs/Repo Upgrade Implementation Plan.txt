Comprehensive Architectural Blueprint and Concrete Implementation Roadmap for the Next-Generation Environmental Clearance Ecosystem
Executive Synthesis and the Regulatory Imperative
The digitalization of environmental governance represents a profound operational shift, requiring a delicate synchronization between rigid regulatory compliance, bureaucratic efficiency, and stringent ecological safeguarding.1 The baseline requirements set forth by the Chhattisgarh Environment Conservation Board (CECB) under Problem Statement 02 mandate the development of a unified, role-based web portal capable of managing the comprehensive lifecycle of an environmental clearance application.1 This lifecycle spans the initial application filing by a Project Proponent (PP) or Recognized Qualified Professional (RQP), rigorous regulatory verification by a Scrutiny Team, and the finalization of the Minutes of Meeting (MoM) by a dedicated MoM Team.1 Applications must progress linearly through distinct stages: Draft, Submitted, Under Scrutiny, Essential Document Sought (EDS), Referred, MoM Generated, and Finalized.1
The broader macroeconomic and regulatory context in India, as highlighted by recent central government initiatives and the Economic Survey 2025-26, emphasizes a transition from traditional command-and-control frameworks toward trust-based, technology-enabled regulation.2 The national PARIVESH 3.0 initiative exemplifies this paradigm shift, functioning as a single-window portal for integrated environmental clearances that heavily leverages Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Geographic Information Systems (GIS) to streamline the ease of doing business while simultaneously tightening environmental audit rules.2 Consequently, an acceptable technological solution cannot merely digitize paper forms; it must actively enforce compliance and automate analytical reasoning.1
Historically, legacy government portals and rapid hackathon prototypes—such as the baseline state of the repository Aqwerty321/ecoclearworkflow_hackthon—rely on tightly coupled, synchronous monolithic architectures that facilitate basic Create, Read, Update, Delete (CRUD) operations.1 While these architectures allow for rapid initial prototyping, they inevitably succumb to severe scaling bottlenecks, single points of failure, and deployment friction as system complexity expands.1 Furthermore, conventional platforms treat data passively, relying entirely on human operators to manually verify thousands of pages of heterogeneous documents and manually audit self-reported spatial coordinates.1
To definitively solve the CECB problem statement and align with the trajectory of PARIVESH 3.0, the proposed architectural upgrade dictates an enterprise-grade, highly decoupled paradigm.1 By cross-validating the baseline workflow constraints against advanced deployment architectures, it becomes evident that integrating Event-Driven Architecture (EDA), Agentic Artificial Intelligence via LangGraph, PostGIS spatial analysis, Conflict-Free Replicated Data Types (CRDTs), and Hyperledger Fabric establishes an unassailable e-governance platform.1 This exhaustive research report analyzes the concrete technological implementations required for each domain and provides a highly specific foundational roadmap to transition the existing repository into a production-ready, highly observable, and legally defensible regulatory engine.1
Foundational Infrastructure: Event-Driven Microservices and Distributed State Management
Legacy environmental portals frequently succumb to performance degradation and catastrophic failure during periods of high traffic.1 This fragility is rooted in synchronous request-response architectures. Given the complex cascade of actions required upon an environmental clearance application submission—such as massive document virus scanning, AI parsing, and extensive spatial cross-referencing—a synchronous thread would block the main application, resulting in severe latency and a degraded user experience.1
The Imperative for Asynchronous Decoupling
The proposed system must adopt an Event-Driven Architecture (EDA) utilizing asynchronous microservices orchestrated via robust enterprise message brokers, such as Apache Kafka or RabbitMQ.1 In an environmental clearance lifecycle, a single user action frequently necessitates a multitude of downstream operations.1 By implementing an EDA, the event producer is fundamentally decoupled from the event consumers.1
When a Project Proponent finalizes an application, the API Gateway does not wait for all subsequent processing to complete.1 Instead, it instantly publishes a DocumentSubmitted event to the message broker.1 Independent microservices subscribe to this specific topic and react autonomously.1 This asynchronous execution guarantees that heavy computational tasks, such as generating dense vector embeddings of a 500-page Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) report or fetching multispectral satellite imagery via external APIs, operate in the background.1
A critical structural advantage derived from this architecture is its inherent fault tolerance and scalability. If the AI-based document scrutiny service experiences temporary downtime due to a massive backlog of complex thermal power plant applications, the document ingestion service remains fully operational.1 The message broker persistently queues the analysis events for execution once the consumer service recovers or is dynamically scaled horizontally, ensuring zero data loss and a continuously responsive User Interface.1
Distributed Transactions via the Event-Driven Saga Pattern
Managing the strict linear progression of application states—from Draft through to Finalized—across distributed microservices introduces the profound challenge of maintaining data consistency.1 Traditional ACID (Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, Durability) transactions are generally confined to single relational databases and cannot safely span across decoupled microservices.1 If a multi-step workflow fails midway, the system risks leaving an application in a corrupted, intermediate state.1
To manage these distributed workflows, the architecture implements the Event-Driven Saga design pattern.1 A Saga is a sequence of local transactions where each transaction updates data within a single service and subsequently publishes an event that triggers the next step in the broader workflow.1 Crucially, the Saga pattern gracefully manages partial system failures through automated compensating transactions.1
For example, when a verified application is transitioned to the "Referred" state by the Scrutiny Team, the Saga orchestrator initiates the subsequent step: triggering the automatic meeting gist generation.1 Should the Large Language Model (LLM) powered gist generation module encounter a critical failure—such as an API timeout, rate limit exhaustion, or an out-of-memory error—the Saga autonomously publishes a compensating event.1 This compensating event reverts the application status back to the preceding "Under Scrutiny" state and instantly alerts the system administrator.1 This programmatic rollback mechanism guarantees absolute system integrity, ensuring that the global state remains perfectly consistent and directly fulfilling the evaluation criterion for rigorous "Automation logic".1
Access Governance, Identity Verification, and Cryptographic Security
The CECB problem statement explicitly requires strict role-based access control managed by a central administrator, encompassing dynamic role assignments between the Scrutiny Team and the MoM Team, alongside the provisioning of sector-specific parameters.1 However, baseline Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) utilizing simple database boolean flags and session tokens is insufficient for securing highly sensitive governmental environmental data against modern threat vectors and complex jurisdictional boundaries.1
Transitioning to a Hybrid RBAC-ABAC Framework
The proposed architecture mandates a transition to a Hybrid RBAC-ABAC (Attribute-Based Access Control) model.1 Pure RBAC assigns permissions based exclusively on static roles, which frequently leads to "role explosion" in complex government organizations.1 Administrators are forced to create increasingly granular roles (e.g., Scrutiny_Team_Mining_DistrictA) to accommodate specific edge cases, resulting in unmanageable security matrices.1 Conversely, pure ABAC evaluates multiple conditions simultaneously, which can be computationally intensive to manage at scale for every basic endpoint.1
The hybrid model leverages the strengths of both paradigms. A user may be assigned the broad RBAC role of "Scrutiny Team" for initial authentication and interface rendering.1 However, the ABAC policy engine dynamically restricts their underlying authorization based on specific attributes.1

Integration of the India Stack for Absolute Non-Repudiation
To elevate the prototype into a production-ready Indian GovTech solution, user registration and document finalization must transcend basic email verification and standard database updates.1 The architecture deeply integrates the India Stack, a comprehensive set of open APIs and digital public goods aimed at unlocking identity, data, and payment primitives at a population scale.5
The user registration process for Project Proponents and Recognized Qualified Professionals (RQPs) utilizes Aadhaar-based electronic Know Your Customer (e-KYC) protocols.1 This infrastructure allows the application to programmatically verify the identity of the applicant against the Unique Identification Authority of India (UIDAI) database in real-time.5 This biometric and demographic verification ensures that entities filing for environmental clearances are legally verified, aggressively mitigating the risk of fraudulent applications and synthetic identities.1
Furthermore, the workflow mandates that the MoM Team must "lock finalized MoM to prevent further editing".1 Relying on a simple is_editable = false flag in a PostgreSQL database lacks legal defensibility in environmental litigation scenarios within forums such as the National Green Tribunal (NGT).1 The system must therefore integrate eSign APIs licensed by the Controller of Certifying Authorities (CCA) under the Information Technology Act, 2000.1 Providers such as Protean eGov Technologies (formerly NSDL) act as trusted eSign Service Providers (ESPs) and Certifying Authorities.9
When the MoM team finalizes the meeting minutes, the Node.js backend initiates the eSign flow.1 The official authenticates via Aadhaar e-KYC, triggering the CCA-licensed provider to generate a short-term validity X.509 cryptographic certificate.1 The Node.js application, utilizing SDKs or direct API REST calls via libraries like Axios, transmits the document hash, and the provider applies a legally recognized PKCS#7 digital signature directly to the PDF document.1 This process completely eliminates the logistical complexities of managing physical hardware cryptographic tokens (USB dongles) while ensuring a complete, legally binding audit trail.1 Any subsequent alteration to the PDF, even by a single byte, immediately invalidates the cryptographic signature, establishing absolute non-repudiation for the finalized regulatory decisions.1
Agentic AI and LangGraph Orchestration for Autonomous Scrutiny
The conventional environmental clearance process is severely bottlenecked during the "Under Scrutiny" phase.1 Scrutiny Teams are tasked with manually verifying thousands of pages of heterogeneous documents, validating fee calculations, and cross-referencing complex EIA reports against rigid, sector-specific parameters.1 For instance, a human officer must manually verify the requirement for thermal power plants to maintain specific buffer zones or the mandate for sponge iron plants to adhere to strict particulate emission limits.1 The integration of Agentic Artificial Intelligence fundamentally revolutionizes this workflow, transforming the platform from a passive document repository into an active, intelligent reasoning engine.1
The Necessity of LangGraph over Linear Chains
While simple Large Language Model (LLM) implementations rely on deterministic, linear chains or simple prompt-response loops, these architectures are highly brittle when handling complex, ambiguous regulatory logic.1 Generative models supply the reasoning substrate, but an agentic architecture is required to supply the operational execution and tool orchestration.14 To implement true autonomy, the architecture leverages LangGraph, a sophisticated framework built on top of LangChain, designed specifically for orchestrating cyclic, multi-actor agentic workflows with persistent state management.1
LangGraph models agent workflows as stateful, cyclical directed graphs rather than simple linear pipelines.1 The behavior of these autonomous agents is defined by three core components:
1. State: A shared data structure, often defined via TypedDict or MessagesState in Python, that represents the current snapshot of the application, managing conversation history and intermediate outputs across the entire workflow.1
2. Nodes: Python functions that encode the specific logic of the agents, receiving the state, performing computation, reasoning, or utilizing external tools, and returning an updated state.1
3. Edges: Conditional routing functions that determine which Node to execute next based on the dynamic state, allowing for complex branching and looping.1
This graph algorithm allows the program to proceed in discrete "super-steps" where the AI can iteratively evaluate its own outputs.1 This cyclical reflection is critical; unlike linear models that propagate errors downstream, LangGraph enables a system to critique its own outputs and loop back upstream for iterative correction, dramatically neutralizing hallucination risks.1

	Advanced Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) Infrastructure
The efficacy of the Agentic AI relies entirely on the precision of its information retrieval.1 "Naive RAG" architectures—which simply divide documents into arbitrary character counts and retrieve them based on basic vector similarity—frequently fail in legal contexts because they destroy semantic boundaries and struggle to differentiate between highly nuanced regulatory clauses.1
To precisely process complex CECB sector-specific parameters, the platform implements an Advanced RAG architecture 1:
* Context-Aware Semantic Chunking: The ingestion pipeline utilizes structural parsers to logically divide EIA reports and regulatory documents by section headers (e.g., "Air Quality Mitigation," "Biodiversity Impact") rather than rigid token counts, preserving the contextual integrity of the law.1
* Hybrid Search Methodologies: The system combines dense vector embeddings, which capture semantic meaning via mathematical cosine similarity, with sparse keyword search algorithms like BM25, which excel at finding exact regulatory codes, specific chemical nomenclatures, or precise quantitative limits.1
* Cross-Encoder Re-ranking: Initial hybrid retrieval often returns a high volume of loosely related chunks. The pipeline employs specialized re-ranking models (such as Cohere Rerank) to evaluate the absolute relevance of each retrieved chunk against the specific query, ensuring that only the most pertinent regulatory evidence is injected into the LLM's context window.1

Geospatial Intelligence: Programmatic Eco-Sensitive Zone Enforcement
The State of Chhattisgarh is defined by its rich biodiversity, housing extensive Tropical Moist Deciduous forests, critical wildlife corridors, and significant mineral reserves.1 The CECB enforces strict spatial parameters to manage industrial impact; for example, highly polluting Red Category industries must maintain rigorous ambient air quality monitoring within buffer zones ranging from 5 to 10 kilometers, and industrial activities are heavily restricted near riverbanks or ecologically fragile areas.1
A critical vulnerability in legacy workflow systems is the reliance on self-reported spatial data.1 Project Proponents submit location coordinates and assert their proximity to ecological features, which Scrutiny Teams must manually verify using disjointed third-party desktop tools.1 PARIVESH 3.0 places a massive emphasis on integrating Geographic Information Systems (GIS) and leveraging satellite analytics to establish an authoritative Decision Support System.1 Integrating automated spatial verification transforms the platform into an impregnable regulatory fortress, permanently neutralizing the risks associated with fraudulent spatial self-reporting.1
Integration of High-Resolution Satellite APIs
When a Project Proponent registers an application, they are required to upload the exact spatial boundaries of the proposed site in standard geospatial formats, predominantly GeoJSON, KML, or Shapefiles.1 The application's backend infrastructure features a dedicated geospatial Python microservice that interfaces programmatically with advanced Earth Observation APIs.1
The primary mechanism relies on the Sentinel-2 API, provided via the Copernicus Data Space Ecosystem (CDSE).1 The Sentinel-2 mission comprises twin polar-orbiting satellites equipped with Multispectral Instruments (MSI) capable of sampling 13 distinct spectral bands, including visible, near-infrared (NIR), and shortwave infrared (SWIR).1 With a high spatial resolution of up to 10 meters and a frequent global revisit rate of approximately 5 days, the API provides the application with near real-time, cloud-filtered imagery of the proposed industrial site.1
Through Python-based scripting utilizing libraries such as sentinelhub and openeo, the microservice dynamically extracts the bounding box (BBox) coordinates of the applicant's GeoJSON.23 It then passes this coordinate array to the CDSE Process API to retrieve immediate visual context of the terrain, computing indices like the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) dynamically to assess current land cover.23 Simultaneously, the architecture incorporates sophisticated forward and reverse geocoding APIs (such as Mapbox) to seamlessly translate text-based site addresses into precise longitude and latitude coordinates, providing secondary validation.1
Automated Intersection and Buffer Analysis via PostGIS
Retrieving satellite imagery provides essential visual context, but the platform must autonomously compute ecological risk mathematically.1 The backend database utilizes PostgreSQL augmented with the PostGIS spatial extension to perform complex geometric calculations on the fly.1 PostGIS enables the relational database to natively understand, index, and query complex geographic objects.1
The platform maintains a localized spatial database populated with authoritative GIS layers representing Reserve Forests, Protected Areas, Tiger Reserves, and major hydrological features.1 When the system ingests a GeoJSON polygon representing a new industrial site, the PostGIS engine executes a series of automated spatial algorithms 1:
1. Coordinate Transformation: The ST_Transform function ensures all spatial data (both the uploaded site boundaries and the internal authoritative layers) is projected onto a uniform coordinate reference system (CRS) to prevent geometric alignment errors across large distances.1
2. Dynamic Buffer Generation: The ST_Buffer function dynamically generates regulatory buffer zones around the proposed site polygon. For instance, if the application is for a highly polluting Red Category industry, the database computes a rigorous 10-kilometer buffer radius.1
3. Proximity and Intersection Checks: The ST_Intersects and ST_Distance functions mathematically calculate whether the site polygon, or its generated buffer, encroaches upon any protected geospatial layers.1
If the spatial analysis detects that a proposed project falls within an Eco-Sensitive Zone (ESZ)—which serves as a regulatory "shock absorber" surrounding wildlife sanctuaries—the system autonomously triggers a critical alert.1 These spatial findings are instantly appended to the application's digital dossier alongside the recent Sentinel-2 multispectral imagery.1 This provides the Scrutiny Team with incontrovertible, visual, and mathematical proof of the site's environmental context, allowing them to issue evidence-based rejections or mandate targeted mitigation strategies without requiring immediate physical site inspections or external desktop GIS software.1
Next-Generation Real-Time Collaborative MoM Generation
The culmination of the environmental clearance workflow is the drafting, refinement, and finalization of the Minutes of Meeting (MoM) by the MoM Team.1 The CECB problem statement outlines highly specific technical requirements for this phase: the ability to "trigger automatic meeting gist generation" and the necessity for the MoM Team to "edit and refine generated meeting gist" before final publication.1
Autonomous LLM-Driven Gist Synthesis
Upon an application successfully transitioning to the "Referred" state after Scrutiny validation, the Agentic RAG pipeline is re-engaged to synthesize the historical context.1 The Supervisor LLM aggregates the massive corpus of data accumulated during the lifecycle—the original EIA report, the automated GIS proximity alerts, the AI-generated Scrutiny notes, and any external compliance reports from the SPCB.1
The central administrator provisions predefined, sector-specific master templates for the MoM.1 The LLM intelligently maps the synthesized data into the appropriate sections of these templates.1 It extracts critical parameters such as proposed production capacity, water consumption metrics, and required pollution control equipment, outputting a highly structured, regulatory-compliant initial draft.1 This automation entirely eliminates the tedious administrative burden of manual data collation, allowing the MoM Team to focus immediately on high-level strategic deliberation and policy application.1
Conflict-Free Replicated Data Types (CRDTs) for Real-Time Editing
Fulfilling the "edit and refine" requirement using standard web text areas introduces severe risks of version control conflicts, overwrites, and data loss during concurrent editing sessions among multiple government officials.1 To facilitate enterprise-grade collaboration, the frontend architecture eschews standard WYSIWYG editors in favor of Tiptap, a headless, framework-agnostic rich text editor built upon the ProseMirror library.1
Crucially, Tiptap enables real-time collaboration through deep integration with Yjs, a JavaScript implementation of Conflict-Free Replicated Data Types (CRDTs).1 CRDTs represent a profound mathematical advancement in distributed systems over legacy Operational Transformation (OT) algorithms.1

Establishing Absolute Trust: Immutable Audit Trails via Blockchain
Environmental clearances are frequently the subject of intense legal, public, and regulatory scrutiny.1 Allegations of retroactive document tampering, missing compliance reports, or unauthorized procedural bypasses routinely result in severe litigation.1 Standard relational databases (like SQL Server or PostgreSQL) are fundamentally vulnerable to unauthorized alteration by internal users possessing administrative privileges.1 To unequivocally satisfy the evaluation criteria for "Role based security implementation" and to guarantee absolute data integrity, the system must integrate distributed ledger technology.1
Hyperledger Fabric for Enterprise Governance
Public, permissionless blockchains (such as Ethereum) are fundamentally unsuitable for processing sensitive government data due to intractable issues regarding data privacy, unpredictable transaction latency, and volatile execution costs (gas fees).1 The architecture therefore specifies the integration of Hyperledger Fabric, an enterprise-grade, permissioned distributed ledger framework.1
Hyperledger Fabric employs a unique "execute-order-validate" architecture that fundamentally isolates transaction execution from transaction ordering.1 This architecture utilizes deterministic consensus algorithms (such as Raft) operated by dedicated ordering service nodes, ensuring that any block validated by the peer network is guaranteed to be final and correct; the ledger cannot fork.1 Furthermore, Fabric enables the creation of isolated communication "channels." This allows the CECB to establish confidential multilateral transactions where sensitive proprietary data belonging to a specific corporate Project Proponent is visible only to authorized nodes within their specific channel, while the overarching transaction hashes remain verifiable by the broader network.1
Smart Contracts (Chaincode) and Workflow Tracking
Every critical state transition within the workflow—such as the registration of a new RQP, the issuance of an EDS, the validation of a fee payment, or the final cryptographic locking of the MoM—is executed and recorded immutably on the ledger via smart contracts, known in Fabric as Chaincode.1
Authored in Node.js utilizing the fabric-contract-api, the Chaincode defines the strict business logic governing the environmental clearance process.1 When the Event-Driven Saga triggers an action, a transaction proposal is sent to endorsing peers.1 These peers execute the Node.js Chaincode in isolated Docker containers, verify the user's cryptographic identity against the Membership Service Provider (MSP), and endorse the transaction.1 The transaction is then ordered into a block and committed to the ledger.1 This architecture guarantees that every single administrative action is timestamped, cryptographically signed, and permanently traceable, creating an unassailable audit trail for verifiability.1
Decentralized Document Hashing via IPFS
Storing multi-gigabyte EIA documents and high-resolution satellite imagery directly on a blockchain is computationally inefficient and cost-prohibitive.1 To secure large files, the architecture utilizes a hybrid storage model combining secure Cloud Object Storage with the InterPlanetary File System (IPFS).1
When a document is uploaded to the portal, it is processed through a rigorous cryptographic hashing function (typically SHA-256) to generate a unique Content Identifier (CID).1 The physical document is securely stored in encrypted cloud storage (e.g., AWS S3 with Customer-Managed Encryption Keys), while the generated CID, alongside the uploader's digital signature and timestamp, is recorded immutably via Node.js Chaincode onto the Hyperledger Fabric ledger.1
This hybrid architecture guarantees absolute data provenance. If a submitted document is maliciously or accidentally altered by even a single byte after it has been finalized, its cryptographic hash changes completely.1 This instantly breaks the verification link on the blockchain, immediately exposing the tampering attempt and providing regulators, auditors, and civil society with absolute mathematical proof of document integrity.1

3. Containerization and Infrastructure as Code (IaC)
To deploy this complex matrix of services—Kafka message brokers, PostgreSQL databases with PostGIS extensions, Hyperledger peer nodes, and LangGraph Python servers—the repository requires comprehensive Infrastructure as Code (IaC).1
A robust docker-compose.yml configuration must be added to the repository root to orchestrate the local development environment.1 This configuration must automatically provision the message queues, set up the PostGIS extension on the Postgres container, bootstrap a local Hyperledger Fabric test network (including orderers and endorsing peers), and configure the LLM environment.1 For production deployment, Terraform scripts should be developed to provision scalable cloud infrastructure, manage secure object storage with Customer-Managed Encryption Keys, and handle the networking configurations necessary for secure API gateway routing and load balancing.1
4. Smart Contract Endorsement Policies
The repository must define explicit endorsement policies within the Hyperledger network configuration files.1 The configuration should dictate that a critical state transition (e.g., marking a MoM as "Finalized" and applying the eSign hash) requires cryptographic signatures from multiple endorsing peers representing different organizations (e.g., the CECB, the State Pollution Control Board, and an independent auditor node).1 This multi-signature requirement guarantees that no single rogue node, compromised administrator account, or internal bad actor can unilaterally alter the immutable audit trail, establishing the highest tier of institutional trust.1

The synthesis of the CECB Problem Statement and the exhaustive architectural upgrade strategy yields a blueprint that fundamentally redefines the mechanics of environmental governance.1 By shifting from a paradigm of manual, retrospective auditing to one of automated, proactive verification, the proposed platform guarantees total regulatory compliance while aggressively minimizing bureaucratic friction.1
The foundational integration of Event-Driven Microservices orchestrated via Saga patterns prevents system gridlock during high-throughput ingestion phases, ensuring massive, fault-tolerant scalability.1 LangGraph-orchestrated Agentic AI, functioning under a Supervisor architecture, transforms the Scrutiny Team's role, autonomously generating the Essential Document Sought (EDS) and identifying complex regulatory deficiencies with unmatched precision.1 Furthermore, the integration of PostGIS and Sentinel-2 satellite APIs enables the platform to algorithmically verify spatial proximity to eco-sensitive zones, permanently neutralizing the risks associated with fraudulent spatial self-reporting.1
The execution of real-time collaborative MoM drafting via CRDTs eradicates version control nightmares, and the subsequent cryptographic locking of these documents via CCA-licensed eSign APIs and Hyperledger Fabric Chaincode establishes an unassailable, legally defensible audit trail.1 Finally, deep-linked UPI intent flows coupled with webhook reconciliation ensure that financial processing operates seamlessly without human intervention.1
By implementing the recommended repository interventions—specifically transitioning to a microservices monorepo, enforcing AI observability, and establishing robust Infrastructure as Code pipelines—the prototype transcends hackathon constraints.1 The resulting platform establishes a definitive benchmark for the future of national e-governance, delivering an impregnable, transparent, and highly performant regulatory ecosystem capable of powering the ambitions of PARIVESH 3.0.1
